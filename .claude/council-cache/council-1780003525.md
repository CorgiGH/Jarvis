The prior council's sharpest finding was behavioral, not architectural: building the system is avoidance of studying, and the dev is a first-year student who can't vet correctness. None of the refinements address either — and the timeline makes it acute: finals are days away in early June. Re-orienting to build a brand-new teaching engine from scratch (shape classifier, mode router, prereq-aware video graph, learner profiles) in the final pre-exam week is the avoidance pattern at maximum intensity, wearing a more defensible spec. The "no un-cited claim / 'I don't know' beats confident-wrong" discipline is unverifiable by this builder: a first-year can't detect when a real citation is subtly MIS-taught — the failure isn't fabricated citations, it's plausible-but-mistaught correct sources, which only a domain expert catches.
KEY CONCERN: Same avoidance-of-studying flaw the prior council named, executed in the worst possible week — a from-scratch engine build launched days before the exams it's meant to help pass, by the one person who needs those days to study and cannot independently verify the engine teaches correctly.

Reusing FSRS + a rubric grader is textbook spaced-repetition + mastery learning (Anki-FSRS, Bloom's 2-sigma loop) — verified both exist in the tree (FsrsCards.kt, DrillGrader.kt), as does the verbatim-citation guard. Pick an EXPLICIT mastery rule (even a thresholded EWMA of graded attempts per KC, prereq-gated unlocking) — else FSRS schedules cards while nothing actually decides "this student has mastered KC-003," and the mastery-learning claim is unbacked.
