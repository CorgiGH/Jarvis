## 🔴 Devil's Advocate
AGENT: Devil's Advocate
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The three mitigations all defend against fabrication, but the real failure mode is undetectable miscalibration: a grounded, well-pedagogized, Claude-generated explanation that is subtly WRONG (right citation, wrong inference from it) sails past every guardrail because the one person who could catch it — Alex — explicitly cannot vet subject correctness, and you've already proven this session that real models false-reject good drills AND emit self-inconsistent grading. Grounding stops invention but not misinterpretation; "known pedagogy" makes a wrong explanation MORE convincing and stickier (predict-then-reveal cements the error, I-do/we-do/you-do drills it into FSRS, and spaced repetition then schedules the wrong belief for lifelong reinforcement). Worse, the deterministic layer's response to uncertainty is to DEFER — which on a tutoring (not grading) surface degrades to silence or "I don't know," so as confidence drops the system doesn't teach wrong, it stops teaching, quietly starving curriculum coverage right where the material is hardest. The mitigations turn "obviously broken" into "looks flawless and is occasionally, invisibly, durably wrong," which for a sole learner with finals June 1-21 is the more dangerous outcome.
KEY CONCERN: No closed-loop correctness check on TAUGHT claims — quote-grounding verifies provenance, not that the inference drawn from the quote is valid, and the only human able to catch a plausible-but-wrong taught claim is definitionally unable to; without an independent verification path (e.g. cross-model adversarial check that the explanation's INFERENCE follows from its cited quote, plus a hard rule that an unverifiable concept is flagged-visible rather than silently deferred), spaced repetition will durably encode rare invisible errors right before high-stakes exams.

## 📚 Domain Expert
AGENT: Domain Expert
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The three mitigations map onto established good ITS practice, and — unusually for a vision pitch — they are real code, not slideware. Grounding every student-facing claim in verbatim lecture quotes (content/PA/kcs/*.yaml source quotes + ContentValidator/CitationExtractor) is exactly retrieval-augmented generation with a closed corpus, the standard mitigation for LLM hallucination, and "I don't know > confident-wrong" is the abstention discipline serious RAG tutors enforce. The deterministic scoring layer (GradeScoring.isConfident defers when the LLM's correct verdict contradicts its own rubric booleans) is the right answer to LLM-grader unreliability — it mirrors how Cognitive Tutor / ASSISTments never let a stochastic judgment silently mutate the learner model, and pairing it with FSRS for scheduling is correct. The pedagogy (I-do/we-do/you-do = worked-example fading / completion problems, predict-then-reveal = the generation/self-explanation effect) is borrowed from the literature rather than invented, which is what the prior council demanded. So four of the five stacked risks are genuinely retired.
KEY CONCERN: The one risk the mitigations do NOT retire is the ITS authoring bottleneck — historically ~200-300 author-hours per instructional hour for Cognitive Tutor, and the reason most ITS die. Grounding fixes truth but not pedagogical coverage: a KC corpus needs a knowledge-component model with prerequisite edges, and critically a misconception library (the wrong mental models a learner holds) to drive Socratic remediation and targeted feedback — yet content/PA/misconceptions/ has only 2 entries and every other subject's misconceptions/ is an empty .gitkeep. Without populated misconceptions and validated KC-graph edges per subject, the system grounds correct statements but cannot diagnose why Alex is wrong or remediate adaptively — it degrades to a grounded explainer + grader, not the adaptive professor in the vision. Since Alex is a solo builder who cannot vet subject correctness, the realistic move is to let Claude mine misconceptions from the same lecture corpus (so they inherit the grounding guarantee) rather than invent them, and treat per-subject misconception + edge coverage as the explicit gate on calling any subject "tutorable."

## ⚙️ Pragmatist
AGENT: Pragmatist
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The mitigations are sound in principle but they trade away the one resource a solo ADHD builder can't spare: operational stability. Routing all student-facing output through the Claude-Max relay means the tutor's core loop now depends on a machine being awake, a throttle ceiling you've already hit, and a false-reject rate you've already measured — so every drill the learner sees is one relay hiccup away from a blank screen or a silently-tossed good problem, and you'll be hand-debugging that at 11pm during finals. The full vision (auto-detect → curriculum → teach → generate → grade → spaced-rep, all personalized) is five coupled subsystems; even with guardrails that's a maintenance surface no single person keeps green while it also has to WORK for a real exam next month. The proportional move is to NOT build the whole professor: pick the one loop with the highest finals-ROI (almost certainly generate+grade over already-curated KCs, since teaching and auto-curriculum are the riskiest and least verifiable by a builder who can't vet content), make THAT loop bulletproof against relay flakiness, and let the rest stay manual.
KEY CONCERN: Relay-dependence is a single point of failure for the live tutor — without an offline/cached fallback path (pre-generate and persist drills + grades so the learner-facing loop never blocks on a real-time relay call), the system is most likely to break exactly when finals make it matter most.

## 🧱 First Principles
AGENT: First Principles
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The raw goal is not "be tutored" — it is for Alex to walk into finals with durable, retrievable knowledge he can produce alone under exam conditions; "a tutor that does all the lifting" is his proposed mechanism, and it is partly self-defeating because the one thing that cannot be outsourced is the effortful retrieval and struggle that actually builds memory (the testing effect / desirable difficulty). A system that "does everything a 1:1 professor does" is fine, but the best 1:1 professors do NOT do the lifting — they engineer the student's lifting: they ask, wait, let him fail safely, then correct; the danger in the framing is that "do all the lifting" optimizes for Alex's in-the-moment comfort (fluency illusion — feeling he understands because the explanation was smooth) rather than his exam-day independence, and ADHD makes the path-of-least-effort pull stronger, so a frictionless tutor risks producing confident recognition that collapses into blank recall on June 1. The grounding/capable-model/known-pedagogy mitigations correctly solve the correctness failure mode (which matters acutely since Alex can't vet output), but they do not address the effort-displacement failure mode at all — you can ground every claim in lecture material and still hand him answers he should have retrieved himself.
KEY CONCERN: The mitigations protect against teaching him wrong things but not against teaching him too easily — the tutor must be architected to force HIS retrieval (generate-before-reveal, withhold the answer until he attempts, schedule recall not re-reading) and measure success by unaided exam-condition performance, not by smoothness of the session; "does all the lifting" must be redefined as "does all the lifting EXCEPT the retrieval, which it instead provokes and scores."

## ⚠️ Risk Analyst
AGENT: Risk Analyst
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The single most likely silent harm is a wrong drill that passes the critic and gets taught as truth to a learner who can't self-vet — and the proposed mitigations leave a clean path for it. Grounding closes the "fabricated fact" hole only as far as extraction is clean, but the spec itself admits the validator checks quotes against MACHINE-EXTRACTED text and some PDFs extract as garbage; on those subjects a drill can be "grounded" in garbled source and still validate, so grounding silently degrades to zero exactly where it's needed most, with no signal to Alex. Compounding it: the grader's own self-inconsistency this session caused FALSE-rejects of good drills, which means the inverse — a self-consistent-but-wrong rubric that PASSES a bad drill — is equally within the model's failure envelope, and "I don't know" deferral only fires when the model knows it's wrong, which by construction it does not here. Claude-as-author plus known-pedagogy reduce raw error rate but neither detects the residual confident-wrong drill, so the failure is rare, silent, and uncatchable rather than eliminated.
KEY CONCERN: CRITICAL — a grounded-on-garbled-extraction or critic-passed wrong drill is taught confidently with zero downstream catch. Mitigations make it RARER, not VISIBLE. To reach APPROVE, gate teaching on extraction quality: per-PDF extraction-confidence score, and any concept whose source quote sits in a low-confidence/garbled region is flagged "unverified — do not teach as fact" rather than silently taught; plus log every critic-passed drill for periodic spot-audit so the residual wrong-but-passed case is at least discoverable.

Add an independent cross-model adversarial check that the taught inference follows from its cited quote; surface "unverifiable" visibly rather than silently deferring. (Devil's Advocate.)

Redefine the vision as "do everything EXCEPT the retrieval, which it provokes and scores" (withhold answer until he attempts; schedule recall, not re-reading; measure unaided performance).
