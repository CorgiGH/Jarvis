## 🔴 Devil's Advocate
AGENT: Devil's Advocate
STANCE: REJECT
REASONING: The spec/spine asks the SYSTEM to do all verification because Alex cannot — yet the verification net is a `confidence` flag and a regex/SymPy/citation chain that ALL run over LLM-generated content (definition plates, worked examples, misconception refutations, grader rules) that does NOT exist yet and that no human will ever check. `content/` proves it: PA has one lecture, 6 real KCs, 2 misconceptions; PA-KC-001 carries only `source` quotes — zero `stem_template`, zero `grader_rules`, zero `invariant`, zero `figureSpec` — and ALO/PS/POO/SO-RC are completely empty. So every Phase-3 "Abstract formal def+invariant," every Phase-4 refutation, and every grading verdict the student is mastery-gated against will be LLM-synthesized at runtime by a free `:free` model, grounded only against the same lecture PDF the LLM extracted it from (circular grounding: the citation net confirms the claim matches the source it was generated from, not that it is pedagogically correct). When that pipeline emits a subtly-wrong invariant or grades a correct answer "wrong," the 5-phase loop does the worst possible thing: it confidently mastery-advances or FSRS-re-queues Alex on the falsehood, the closed-book Retrieve phase trains the error into long-term memory via the testing effect (d=0.61), and the confidence-before-reveal step actively teaches him to feel CERTAIN about the wrong thing. The one human who could catch it is, by the project's founding premise, the one person who provably cannot.
KEY CONCERN: The design treats "content extracted with source spans + a verification net" as equivalent to "verified-correct content," but every link in that net validates faithfulness-to-source or arithmetic, never pedagogical/conceptual correctness — and the only conceptual oracle is an unverifiable novice. A mastery-gated, retrieval-drilled, confidence-calibrated loop is the most efficient possible machine for cementing a silent extraction error into a student who has no way to detect it. Before any of the 29 surfaces ship, there must be a human-vetted (not Alex) correctness gate on the KC corpus for at least the subjects being taught.

## 📚 Domain Expert
AGENT: Domain Expert
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The spine matches known-good ITS practice on most axes: the L0–L4 feedback ladder with give-up friction and a ≥10-attempt wheel-spin trip is exactly Cognitive Tutor's graduated-hint model plus VanLehn's "floundering" detection and Bjork's desirable-difficulties; mastery-gated phase advance is Bloom mastery-learning / ALEKS knowledge-space gating; FSRS-6 + PFA is the right spaced-repetition + Pavlik-Anderson mastery stack; predict-then-reveal is the testing effect (Roediger & Karpicke, d≈0.61) operationalized correctly; confidence-rated-before-reveal is textbook metacognitive calibration (Hacker/Dunlosky) and the calibration plot closes that loop; CRA concrete-before-abstract (Bruner) and Posner conceptual change in Elaborate are real and well-placed. **But the pretest-first ordering is wrong for a true zero-knowledge novice and the doc never names the effect that says so.** Sweller's worked-example effect and Renkl-Atkinson's *faded* worked examples show that for learners with no schema, unsupported problem-solving first imposes pure extraneous cognitive load and produces *less* learning than studying a worked example first; the productive-failure literature (Kapur) that would justify pretest-first requires enough prior knowledge to generate varied solution attempts — which Alex, by definition, does not have. The expertise-reversal effect (Kalyuga) is the other half: worked-examples-first helps the novice and *reverses* to problem-solving-first only as mastery rises — the scaffold should be a function of the PFA mastery state the system already computes, not a uniform shell.
KEY CONCERN: For a genuine novice the loop should open with a faded worked example (study → completion problem → full problem), not a cold pretest, and the open→problem fade should be keyed to PFA mastery (expertise-reversal). Secondary fixable gaps: (1) **interleaving is absent** — the queue picks the single next card but nothing mandates interleaving KCs/problem-types; Rohrer-Taylor interleaving is one of the largest durable-learning + discrimination effects (it teaches "which method applies" = an exam skill); Thompson sampling here optimizes prompt variants, not concept-sequence interleaving. (2) **Transfer / far-transfer unaddressed** — template-instantiated drills risk teaching template-matching the exam's novel phrasing defeats. (3) ADHD fit is mostly right, but gate the *number* of phases by entry mastery, not just transition thresholds.

## ⚠️ Risk Analyst
AGENT: Risk Analyst
STANCE: CONDITIONAL
REASONING: The top risk is CRITICAL and it is the unverifiable-correctness chain at the *corpus-authoring* layer, not the runtime Q&A layer the 5-layer defense actually guards. The defenses are real but all live on the live-generation path (PaperQA2 citation refusal, cross-family generator-critic, SymPy, "nu sunt sigur" suppress); the durable KC corpus is built by `KCExtractFromPDF`/`MisconceptionExtract` whose only gate is "Alex swipes accept/reject in a Tinder queue" — and the entire premise is that Alex cannot tell a subtly-wrong definition, invariant, or refutation from a right one. A confidently-worded extraction error (wrong loop invariant, off-by-one complexity class, an inverted Bayes step) passes the swipe, is written to gold YAML, becomes the grader's reference, and is then deliberately burned into long-term memory by FSRS-6 and possibly fanned out to 3-30 classmates' shared cold-start corpus — maximum blast radius, slowest to detect. SymPy explicitly "conceptual errors not caught." Second-worst: the same chain fails *silent* on grading — "grading determinism" guarantees deterministic, not *correct*; a deterministically-wrong `grader_rules` marks a correct answer wrong every time, and the only signal is `REPORT WRONG`, which depends on the one user who can't recognize the error.
KEY CONCERN: The extraction/grading-correctness oracle problem. Citation-grounding proves a claim is traceable to a span, not that extraction preserved the span's meaning (drop a "not", swap ≤ for <, attach a refutation to the wrong KC — all still "cited"). Before this drives FSRS or multi-user, add: (a) cross-family extraction-critic that independently re-derives each KC's definition/invariant/grader-rule and must agree, disagreements quarantined OUT of Alex's swipe queue; (b) back-translation/round-trip check span↔claim by a different model family; (c) FSRS quarantine — no KC enters spaced-rep or cohort cold-start until it clears N independent correct human attempts OR a second-model audit; (d) grading fail-safe: every "wrong" verdict cross-checked by the critic before shown, and a single `REPORT WRONG` hard-pauses FSRS for that KC. Without (c) the system's highest-confidence error is also its most durable.

VERDICT: FLAWED

CORE FINDING:
The teaching LOOP itself is close to best-practice — the L0-L4 ladder, mastery gating, FSRS, predict-then-reveal, and confidence calibration are all textbook-correct ITS and well-applied. But "is this the BEST way to teach Alex?" cannot be answered yes, for one reason all five agents independently reached: the system can silently teach him something WRONG and he is the one person who can never catch it — and that error is then maximally cemented by the very mechanisms that make the loop good (mastery-advance + spaced-repetition + confidence-calibration burn a hallucinated invariant into durable memory). That is a foundational correctness flaw, not a UI flaw, and the design's "verification net" does not address it — every link checks faithfulness-to-source or arithmetic, never conceptual correctness. Secondarily, the effort is mis-sequenced: the engine largely exists and 4 of 5 subjects have NO content, so polishing 29 UI surfaces is scaffolding around the wrong fulcrum.

AGENT CONSENSUS: 2 REJECT (Devil's Advocate, First Principles), 3 CONDITIONAL (Domain Expert, Pragmatist, Risk Analyst) — 0 flagged; all 5 converged on the same core (unverifiable-correctness + content gap).

KEY ISSUES:
1. **Correctness oracle gap (CRITICAL).** Source-citation ≠ conceptual correctness. With Alex as the only oracle and FSRS as the amplifier, the highest-confidence error becomes the most durable one. The swipe-accept queue routes verification through exactly the person who can't verify.
2. **Pretest-first is wrong for a true novice.** Worked-example effect + faded examples + expertise-reversal say: open with a worked example, fade to problem-solving as mastery rises — keyed to the PFA state the system already has. The uniform shell teaches the highest-load step first to the learner least able to bear it.
3. **Missing: interleaving + transfer probes.** The queue serves one card at a time but never interleaves problem-types (Rohrer-Taylor = the "which method applies" exam skill) and never probes far-transfer, risking template-matching the exam defeats.
4. **Mis-sequenced effort.** Engine ~exists; content for 4/5 subjects is empty. UI polish is displacement from the real gap.

RECOMMENDED PATH:
Don't proceed UI-first. Fix in this order:
- **#1 (highest value): build a correctness gate that does NOT route through Alex** — independent cross-family re-derivation of each KC's definition/invariant/grader-rule (disagreements quarantined OUT of his queue), a span↔claim round-trip check by a different model family, and **FSRS quarantine** (no KC enters spaced-rep or the cohort corpus until it clears an independent audit or N correct attempts). A `REPORT WRONG` hard-pauses FSRS for that KC. This converts the system from "fails silent" to "fails safe."
- **Then content, not surfaces:** hand-author/verify a thin KC spine for the NEXT real exam first; defer Thompson/PFA-phase-progression/GEPA/multi-user/Postgres/voice until after finals.
- **Cheap design fixes to fold in:** open the loop with a faded worked example for low mastery (not a cold pretest); add interleaving to the queue; add a transfer-probe variant. Gate the NUMBER of phases by entry mastery.
